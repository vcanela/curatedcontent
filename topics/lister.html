Joseph Lister & Sterilisation

How one surgeon's application of germ theory transformed the operating theatre — and made modern surgery possible.

Topic Overview

Before Joseph Lister introduced antiseptic surgery in the 1860s, around half of all surgical patients died from post-operative infections. Surgery was a last resort — not because of the procedure itself, but because of what happened after. Infection was considered an inevitable part of healing.

Inspired by Louis Pasteur's germ theory, Lister began using carbolic acid (phenol) to sterilise surgical instruments, dress wounds, and clean operating environments. Mortality rates in his wards dropped dramatically. His methods, though initially resisted by many surgeons, eventually transformed surgical practice worldwide and laid the foundation for modern sterile technique.

Key Questions to Consider

  • Why did some surgeons refuse to adopt Lister's methods even after his results were published?
  • How does Lister's story connect to Semmelweis's?
  • What is the difference between antiseptic and aseptic technique?
