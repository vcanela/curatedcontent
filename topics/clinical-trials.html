How Clinical Trials Work

Understanding how medical treatments are tested helps you evaluate health claims — and recognise when a source is misrepresenting the evidence.

Topic Overview

When someone claims that a treatment "works," how do we know if that's true? Clinical trials are the gold standard for testing medical interventions. A well-designed trial controls for bias, uses comparison groups, and measures outcomes systematically — producing the kind of evidence that medical guidelines are built on.

Understanding clinical trials also helps you become a more critical reader of health information. Claims like "studies show" or "scientists say" can mean very different things depending on how the study was designed, how large it was, and whether it has been independently replicated. This topic explores those distinctions.

  • What is a control group and why is it necessary?
  • What does "double-blind" mean and why does it reduce bias?
  • Why is one study usually not enough to draw a conclusion?
  • What is the difference between correlation and causation?
