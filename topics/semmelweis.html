Semmelweis & Handwashing

The story of a doctor who proved handwashing saved lives — and was ridiculed for it. A case study in how scientific progress can be resisted even by scientists.

Topic Overview

In 1847, Hungarian physician Ignaz Semmelweis discovered that requiring doctors to wash their hands with chlorinated lime solution dramatically reduced deaths from childbed fever (puerperal fever) in the maternity wards of Vienna General Hospital. His data was compelling. The establishment refused to accept it.

Semmelweis died in a mental institution in 1865, largely unrecognised. It was not until Louis Pasteur and Joseph Lister developed germ theory that the medical world accepted what Semmelweis had demonstrated statistically two decades earlier. His story raises important questions about how science progresses — and what gets in the way.

Key Questions to Consider

  • Why did doctors resist Semmelweis's findings even when the data was clear?
  • What does this story tell us about how science changes over time?
  • What is the difference between a theory and a proven fact?
  • Can you think of modern examples where good evidence was initially dismissed?
