Antibiotics: Mechanisms, History, and the Resistance Crisis

AuthorsDr. Hemi Ngata1, Dr. Claire Sutherland2
Affiliations1School of Biological Sciences, Victoria University of Wellington; 2Institute for Environmental Science and Research (ESR), Porirua
Received4 October 2023
Accepted19 January 2024 (following peer review)
Published7 March 2024
DOI10.1186/jame.2024.0312

Abstract

Antibiotics have been among the most transformative medicines in human history, reducing mortality from bacterial infections dramatically over the twentieth century. This review article covers the mechanisms by which antibiotics act on bacterial cells, the historical development of antibiotic classes from Fleming's 1928 discovery of penicillin through to contemporary drug development, and the global crisis of antimicrobial resistance (AMR). The article also addresses the role of antibiotic stewardship in preserving the efficacy of existing agents and outlines the New Zealand context for AMR surveillance and policy.

"The time may come when penicillin can be bought by anyone in the shops. Then there is the danger that the ignorant man may easily underdose himself and by exposing his microbes to non-lethal quantities of the drug make them resistant." — Alexander Fleming, Nobel Lecture, 1945

1. What Are Antibiotics?

Antibiotics are compounds — originally derived from living organisms, now often synthesised chemically — that inhibit the growth of bacteria or kill them outright. The term was coined in 1942 by Selman Waksman, who defined an antibiotic as "a chemical substance produced by microorganisms which has the capacity to inhibit the growth of and even destroy bacteria and other microorganisms."[1]

It is important to note that antibiotics have no effect on viruses. Viral illnesses such as colds, influenza, and COVID-19 do not respond to antibiotic treatment. Prescribing antibiotics for viral infections is one of the principal drivers of antimicrobial resistance.[2]

2. Mechanisms of Action

Antibiotics exploit structural and biochemical features that are unique to bacteria — or that differ sufficiently from human cells — to selectively damage bacterial cells while leaving human cells relatively unharmed.

MechanismEffect on BacteriaAntibiotic Class Example
Cell wall synthesis inhibitionBacteria cannot maintain their cell wall; they swell and burstPenicillins, cephalosporins, vancomycin
Protein synthesis inhibition (30S ribosome)Disrupts translation of mRNA; prevents protein productionAminoglycosides, tetracyclines
Protein synthesis inhibition (50S ribosome)Prevents elongation of protein chainsMacrolides, chloramphenicol
DNA replication inhibitionBlocks enzymes needed to unwind and replicate DNAFluoroquinolones
Cell membrane disruptionDestabilises bacterial cell membrane, causing leakagePolymyxins

Human cells lack cell walls, and our ribosomes and DNA-replication enzymes differ structurally from bacterial equivalents — which is why antibiotics targeting these structures are selectively toxic to bacteria.[3]

3. A Brief History of Antibiotic Development

Fleming and Penicillin (1928)

Alexander Fleming's observation that a Penicillium mould was killing bacterial colonies on an agar plate is one of the most celebrated accidental discoveries in science. Fleming identified the active compound — penicillin — but was unable to purify it for clinical use. That breakthrough came from Howard Florey and Ernst Chain at Oxford in 1940, leading to mass production for the Second World War and a Nobel Prize shared by all three in 1945.[4]

The Golden Age (1940s–1970s)

Between 1940 and 1970, the pharmaceutical industry introduced most of the antibiotic classes still in clinical use today: streptomycin (1943), chloramphenicol (1947), tetracyclines (1948), erythromycin (1952), vancomycin (1958), and many others. This period transformed medical outcomes — previously fatal infections including tuberculosis, syphilis, and bacterial meningitis became treatable.[5]

The Innovation Gap (1980s–present)

New antibiotic classes have been extremely rare since the 1980s. Between 1962 and 2000, no new classes of antibiotics were approved by the US Food and Drug Administration. The pipeline remains thin: developing a new antibiotic is costly, time-consuming, and commercially unattractive because effective antibiotics are used sparingly to preserve their efficacy.[6]

4. Antimicrobial Resistance

Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) occurs when bacteria evolve mechanisms to survive antibiotic treatment. This is an entirely natural evolutionary process — selection pressure from antibiotic exposure favours bacteria that carry resistance mutations or resistance genes. The problem is that human overuse of antibiotics has dramatically accelerated this process.[7]

Resistance Mechanisms

Bacteria have evolved multiple ways to resist antibiotics: producing enzymes that break down the antibiotic (e.g., beta-lactamases that deactivate penicillins); modifying the antibiotic's target so the drug no longer binds effectively; pumping the antibiotic out of the cell via efflux pumps; and changing cell membrane permeability to prevent the antibiotic entering.[8]

Resistance genes can also be transferred between bacteria horizontally — a bacterium does not need to "invent" resistance itself, but can acquire resistance genes from other bacterial species through a process called horizontal gene transfer.[9]

The Global Burden

The WHO's 2023 Global Antimicrobial Resistance and Use Surveillance System report estimated that bacterial AMR was directly responsible for 1.27 million deaths globally in 2019, and a contributing factor in a further 4.95 million. If current trends continue, AMR could become the leading cause of death globally by 2050.[10]

5. Antibiotic Stewardship

Antibiotic stewardship refers to coordinated strategies to optimise antibiotic use — prescribing the right antibiotic at the right dose for the right duration, and only when genuinely needed. In New Zealand, the Health Quality and Safety Commission coordinates the national antimicrobial stewardship programme, and community prescribing data is monitored through the Pharmaceutical Management Agency (PHARMAC).[11]

Key stewardship principles include: not prescribing antibiotics for viral infections; completing prescribed courses; and avoiding sharing or saving antibiotics for later use.[11]
