Ignaz Semmelweis and the Origins of Infection Control

How a Hungarian physician demonstrated the role of hand hygiene in preventing hospital-acquired infection — and was rejected by the medical establishment of his time

Author: Professor Sarah Kimura, History of Medicine, University of Auckland First published: 2018 Last revised: March 2024 Peer reviewed: Yes
Born1 July 1818, Buda, Hungary
Died13 August 1865, Vienna (aged 47)
FieldObstetrics; Hospital hygiene
InstitutionVienna General Hospital; St. Rochus Hospital, Budapest
Key contributionDemonstrated handwashing reduced puerperal fever mortality
RecognitionLargely posthumous; now regarded as a founder of infection control

Background: Puerperal Fever and Hospital Medicine

In the mid-nineteenth century, puerperal fever — also called childbed fever — was a leading cause of maternal death in European hospitals. Women who gave birth in hospital were dying at rates that terrified the public and baffled physicians. In some wards, mortality rates exceeded 10–15% in bad months.[1]

The prevailing medical theories attributed the disease to "miasmas" (bad air), constitutional weakness in the patient, or the effects of shame and fear. No effective treatment was known. The deaths were widely regarded as an unfortunate inevitability of hospital-based childbirth.[2]

Semmelweis's Investigation at Vienna General Hospital

In 1846, Ignaz Semmelweis was appointed as an assistant at the First Obstetric Clinic of Vienna General Hospital. The hospital had two maternity wards, and Semmelweis noticed something puzzling: the mortality rate in the First Clinic — staffed by medical students and doctors — was consistently two to three times higher than in the Second Clinic, which was staffed by midwives.[3]

The difference was so striking that women admitted to the hospital would beg to be assigned to the Second Clinic. Some preferred to give birth on the street and be admitted after delivery rather than enter the First Clinic.[4]

The Death of Jakob Kolletschka

In 1847, Semmelweis's colleague and friend Jakob Kolletschka died after being accidentally cut with a scalpel during a post-mortem examination. The symptoms of his subsequent illness were strikingly similar to those of puerperal fever. Semmelweis drew the inference: cadaverous particles transmitted on the hands of medical students and doctors — who moved from performing autopsies directly to delivering babies — were causing the deaths of maternity patients.[5]

The Intervention: Chlorinated Lime Handwashing

Semmelweis introduced a policy requiring all students and doctors to wash their hands with a chlorinated lime solution (calcium hypochlorite) before examining patients. The results were remarkable:

Within months of introducing the handwashing requirement, mortality in the First Clinic fell to match — and then equal — the Second Clinic's rate.[3]

Reception and Rejection

Despite compelling statistical evidence, Semmelweis's findings were largely rejected by the medical establishment. Several factors contributed to this resistance.

Theoretical Framework

Semmelweis could not explain why handwashing worked. Germ theory — the understanding that specific microorganisms cause specific diseases — was not yet established. Without a theoretical mechanism, many physicians found the evidence difficult to accept. Science does not always move forward from data alone; evidence must also fit within a theoretical framework that allows it to be understood.[6]

Professional Identity and Resistance

Semmelweis's hypothesis implied that physicians were themselves responsible for their patients' deaths — that the doctors' unclean hands were killing the women in their care. This was profoundly threatening to medical identity and professional pride. Several senior physicians explicitly rejected the hypothesis on these grounds.[7]

Semmelweis's Communication Style

Historians note that Semmelweis was a poor advocate for his own ideas. He delayed publishing his main work, Die Ätiologie, der Begriff und die Prophylaxis des Kindbettfiebers (Etiology, Concept and Prophylaxis of Childbed Fever), until 1861 — fourteen years after his initial discovery. The work was prolix and disorganised. When critics responded, Semmelweis's replies became increasingly vitriolic. His mental and physical health deteriorated in his final years.[8]

"I must frankly admit that God only knows the number of patients who went prematurely to their graves because of me."

— Ignaz Semmelweis, Etiology, Concept and Prophylaxis of Childbed Fever, 1861

Death and Legacy

In July 1865, Semmelweis was committed to the Döbling mental asylum in Vienna by colleagues. He died there on 13 August 1865, at age 47. The cause of death is debated; some accounts suggest he died of septicaemia — an infection of the blood — potentially caused by a wound sustained during or shortly before his admission. The bitter irony has not been lost on historians.[8]

Within years of his death, Louis Pasteur's germ theory and Joseph Lister's antiseptic surgical techniques provided the theoretical framework Semmelweis had lacked. The medical world accepted what his data had shown. Today Semmelweis is recognised as a foundational figure in hospital hygiene and infection control. His story is frequently cited as a case study in how scientific progress can be delayed by social, professional, and theoretical resistance.[9]

Significance for the History and Philosophy of Science

The Semmelweis case has become central to discussions in the philosophy of science and science studies. It illustrates that empirical evidence alone is rarely sufficient for scientific acceptance — evidence must be accompanied by a coherent explanatory theory, and must survive the social processes of peer review, debate, and institutional endorsement.[10]

The term "Semmelweis reflex" has entered the scientific and sociological lexicon to describe the tendency to reject new evidence or knowledge because it contradicts established norms, beliefs, or paradigms. This concept must, however, be applied carefully: not all resistance to new ideas reflects bias — sometimes evidence genuinely is weak, and scepticism is appropriate.[10]
