The Basics
What Is a Clinical Trial?

A clinical trial is a carefully designed research study that tests new medical treatments — including drugs, devices, vaccines, and procedures — in human volunteers. Clinical trials are the final and most rigorous step in a long research and development process. Every medicine currently available to you went through this process.

Before any treatment reaches clinical testing, it will typically have been studied extensively in laboratory settings and animal models over many years. Only treatments that show genuine promise and an acceptable safety profile reach human trials. And within human trials, there is a structured, staged process designed to ensure that patient safety is the highest priority at every step.

Clinical trials are conducted under strict regulatory oversight. In New Zealand, this means compliance with Medsafe requirements and the oversight of an independent Health and Disability Ethics Committee (HDEC), which reviews every trial to ensure that it is scientifically sound, that participant rights are protected, and that the potential benefits justify any risks.

How Trials Progress
The Four Phases of Clinical Testing

Clinical trials are organised into four phases, each with a specific purpose. Understanding these phases helps you know exactly what stage of development a treatment is at — and what questions are still being answered — before you consider participating.

I
Safety Testing
20–100 participants
The first time a treatment is tested in humans. Researchers focus on safety, dosing, and how the body processes the treatment. Participants receive close medical attention throughout.
II
Efficacy Exploration
100–300 participants
Researchers begin exploring whether the treatment has the desired effect, while continuing to gather safety data. Phase II provides early signals about whether the treatment is promising enough to test further.
III
Confirmation
300–3,000+ participants
Large-scale trials comparing the new treatment to current standard of care or placebo. This is where a treatment's effectiveness and safety profile is comprehensively established. Regulatory approval follows if results are positive.
IV
Post-Market Surveillance
Thousands of patients
Ongoing monitoring after a treatment is approved and available. Tracks long-term safety and effectiveness in the broader population, identifying any rare effects that large trials may not have detected.
Why Participate
The Benefits of Clinical Trial Participation

Many patients who participate in clinical trials describe the experience as a meaningful and positive one. There are several distinct reasons why New Zealanders choose to take part in medical research.

🔬
Access to Cutting-Edge Treatments
Trial participants may gain access to innovative treatments that are not yet available outside the research setting. This can be particularly meaningful for patients with conditions where current treatments are limited.
👩‍⚕️
Close Medical Monitoring
Trial participants receive a higher level of medical attention and monitoring than is typically available in standard clinical care. Your health is closely tracked throughout, and any changes are responded to promptly.
🌍
Advancing Medicine for Everyone
Every participant in a clinical trial contributes to the scientific knowledge that will benefit future patients. Participation is an act of generosity toward the broader community — including people whose lives may depend on the treatments you help test.
💰
No Cost for Trial Treatment
Trial medications, devices, and related procedures are provided to participants at no cost. Many trials also reimburse travel and accommodation expenses associated with appointments.
🤝
Expert Care Team
You will be supported throughout the trial by an experienced research team including doctors, nurses, and coordinators who are specialists in clinical research and patient welfare.
📋
Your Rights Are Protected
Participation is entirely voluntary. You may withdraw at any time without it affecting your access to other medical care. Your rights are protected by ethics committee oversight, informed consent processes, and New Zealand law.
Participant Protection
How Your Safety Is Protected

Patient safety is the cornerstone of clinical research. New Zealand clinical trials operate under a comprehensive framework of protections that exist at every level — from initial design through to long-term follow-up.

Safeguards in Every TrialConnect NZ Partner Study

  • Independent Ethics Committee Review: Every trial must receive approval from a Health and Disability Ethics Committee (HDEC) before recruiting begins. The committee reviews the trial design, participant information, and consent process independently of the sponsor.
  • Independent Data Monitoring Committee: Most Phase III trials include an independent committee that reviews safety data during the trial. This committee has the authority to stop the trial if safety concerns emerge.
  • Medsafe Oversight: New Zealand's medicines regulator monitors clinical trials conducted in New Zealand and can require modifications or suspension of any trial at any time.
  • Participants are carefully monitored for safety throughout the trial using pre-specified monitoring schedules and protocols for responding to adverse events.
  • GCP Compliance: All trials follow International Conference on Harmonisation Good Clinical Practice (ICH-GCP) standards — the global benchmark for ethical and scientific standards in clinical research.
Your Rights
Informed Consent: What It Means for You

Informed consent is the process through which you learn about a clinical trial and decide whether you wish to participate. It is not a single form or a single conversation — it is an ongoing process that continues throughout the trial.

Your trial team is obligated to ensure that you fully understand what the trial involves, what its potential benefits and risks are, what alternatives exist, and what your rights are as a participant. You should feel free to ask any question, at any time. Understanding your role in advancing medical science for future patients is an important part of this conversation.

The Informed Consent Process

Consent is an active, informed decision — not simply a signature. Here is what you can expect:

1
Receive full written information about the trial and read it carefully
2
Have a detailed discussion with the trial team and ask all your questions
3
Take as much time as you need to decide — there is no pressure to decide quickly
4
Sign a consent form confirming your voluntary decision to participate
Common Questions
Frequently Asked Questions
Will I receive the new treatment, or might I get a placebo?
In some trials, participants are randomly assigned to receive either the treatment being tested or a control (placebo or current standard treatment). You will be told whether this is the case in your trial's informed consent documentation. In trials where a placebo is used, you will be informed of this before you consent, and will always be able to receive standard care if needed for your condition.
Can I withdraw from a trial once I've started?
Yes — absolutely. Participation in a clinical trial is entirely voluntary, and you have the right to withdraw at any stage without giving any reason. Withdrawal will never affect your right to access other medical care.
Are there risks involved in participating?
All medical interventions carry some degree of risk, and clinical trials are no exception. Every trial has a risk-benefit assessment that is reviewed by an independent ethics committee before the trial begins. Potential risks will be clearly explained to you during the informed consent process. Participants are carefully monitored for safety throughout, and protocols are in place to respond rapidly to any adverse events.
Who pays for trial treatment and appointments?
Trial medications, devices, and trial-related procedures are provided at no cost to participants. Many trials also offer reimbursement for reasonable travel and accommodation costs associated with trial visits. Your trial coordinator will provide details specific to your trial.
How do I know if a trial is trustworthy?
All clinical trials conducted in New Zealand must be registered on a publicly accessible trials registry and must receive ethical approval from an HDEC. TrialConnect NZ only lists trials that meet these requirements and comply with ICH-GCP standards. You can check the registration of any trial you are interested in on the Australian New Zealand Clinical Trials Registry (ANZCTR).
