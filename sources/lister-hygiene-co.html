The History of Sterile Medicine
Before Lister: Surgery as a Death Sentence

It is difficult to imagine today, but in the middle of the nineteenth century, surviving surgery was more a matter of luck than skill. The most talented surgeons in the world's most advanced hospitals were surrounded by invisible killers they had no knowledge of. Wound infections — what surgeons of the era called "laudable pus," as if suppuration were a natural and necessary part of healing — killed roughly 45 to 50 percent of patients who underwent major operations in British hospitals during the 1860s.

The problem was not surgical technique. The great surgeons of the era were highly skilled. The problem was something no one yet understood: the microscopic world of bacteria. Surgical instruments were not sterilised. Operating theatres were not disinfected between patients. Surgeons wore the same frock coat from one operation to the next, sometimes wearing old dried blood as a badge of experience. The connection between this environment and patient death was entirely invisible to them.

Into this world came a Scottish surgeon named Joseph Lister — and he changed everything.

Lister's Breakthrough
A Glasgow Operating Theatre, 1865
1827
Joseph Lister Born
Joseph Jackson Lister is born in Upton, Essex, England. His father is a pioneering microscopist — an early influence on Lister's scientific thinking.
1860
Appointed to Glasgow
Lister takes up a professorship at the University of Glasgow and begins work at the Royal Infirmary. He is deeply troubled by the catastrophic death rates from post-operative infection.
1865
First Use of Antiseptic Technique
Inspired by Louis Pasteur's germ theory, Lister applies carbolic acid (phenol) to a compound fracture wound. The patient — eleven-year-old James Greenlees — survives without infection. Lister has found his method.
1867
Publication in The Lancet
Lister publishes his findings in five papers in The Lancet, laying out the antiseptic method in detail and documenting its results. The death rate in his wards has fallen dramatically.
1870s–1880s
Antiseptic Technique Spreads
Lister's method is adopted across Europe and North America, though not without resistance. In Germany and Scandinavia, it is taken up rapidly. In England, some surgeons remain sceptical for years.
1912
Lister Dies
Lister dies on 10 February 1912, aged 84. By this time, his principles have transformed surgery worldwide. He is widely regarded as one of the most important medical figures in history.

Lister's insight was to connect Louis Pasteur's recently published germ theory with the problem of post-surgical infection. If microorganisms in the air and environment caused organic matter to decompose and putrefy, might they also be responsible for infecting wounds? And if so, could a chemical agent kill them?

Lister chose carbolic acid — a phenol compound then being used to treat sewage in Carlisle — as his antiseptic agent. He used it on wounds, on dressings, on instruments, and eventually developed a carbolic spray that misted the operating theatre during surgery. The results, documented with meticulous statistical rigour, were unambiguous: post-operative deaths fell dramatically wherever his methods were adopted.

"The next time you wash your hands before a meal, or a surgeon scrubs before an operation, you are participating in a ritual with a 160-year history that begins in a Glasgow operating theatre with a bottle of carbolic acid."

The Science Behind the Breakthrough

What Lister understood — drawing on Pasteur's work — was that disease and putrefaction were caused by living microorganisms, not by "bad air" or miasma as previously believed. This germ theory framework meant that infection could be prevented by killing the microorganisms before they had a chance to enter a wound, rather than simply trying to treat infection after it occurred.

Carbolic acid was effective because it disrupted the cell membranes of bacteria, killing them on contact. Lister's innovation was to apply this chemical systematically: to the wound, the dressings, the instruments, the surgeon's hands, and eventually the air of the operating theatre itself. The concept of creating a "sterile field" — an environment deliberately cleared of potentially infectious microorganisms — was born.

From Carbolic Acid to Modern Sterilisation

Lister's carbolic acid spray is not something you'd find in a modern operating theatre. In fact, prolonged exposure to carbolic acid proved damaging to tissue, and Lister himself eventually moved away from the spray in favour of other antiseptic approaches. But the principle he established — that surgical environments and instruments must be systematically decontaminated — has never been superseded.

What has changed is the sophistication of the methods. Modern hospital sterilisation draws on over 150 years of progressive refinement: autoclaving, gamma irradiation, ethylene oxide sterilisation, and hospital-grade disinfectants specifically formulated against the resistant organisms that matter most in clinical settings.
