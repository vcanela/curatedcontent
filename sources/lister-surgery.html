Biography & Discovery

Joseph Lister and the Development of Antiseptic Surgery

By Dr. Andrew Farnsworth, Senior Research Fellow, Wellcome Institute for the History of Medicine
Published: June 2019 Last reviewed: February 2025 Peer reviewed: Yes Reading level: Secondary/Tertiary
Before Joseph Lister transformed surgical practice in the 1860s, undergoing a major operation was nearly as likely to kill a patient as to save them — not from the surgery itself, but from the infections that followed. Lister's application of Louis Pasteur's germ theory to surgical wounds changed everything.

Surgery Before Lister

In the mid-nineteenth century, post-operative infection — then collectively called "hospital gangrene," "erysipelas," or "septicaemia" depending on its presentation — was an expected consequence of surgery. Surgeons worked in unwashed clothing; instruments were wiped between patients but not sterilised; operating theatres were not disinfected. Speed was considered a surgical virtue partly because longer operations gave "bad air" more time to infect the wound.[1]

Mortality rates for major amputations in the pre-Listerian era were commonly recorded at 40–50%. At some institutions during periods of outbreak, rates exceeded 80%. Surgery was genuinely a last resort.[2]

Lister and Germ Theory

Joseph Lister was born in 1827 in Upton, Essex, to a prosperous Quaker family. His father, Joseph Jackson Lister, was an accomplished amateur scientist who made important improvements to the compound microscope. This exposure to scientific method shaped Lister profoundly.[3]

After qualifying in medicine at University College London and becoming a Fellow of the Royal College of Surgeons in 1852, Lister eventually took up a post as Professor of Surgery at Glasgow University. It was here, in 1865, that he encountered the work of Louis Pasteur, who had demonstrated that fermentation and putrefaction were caused not by spontaneous chemical processes but by living microorganisms.[4]

The key insight: If wound putrefaction (the rotting of surgical wounds) was caused by microorganisms in the same way that Pasteur had shown fermentation to be, then killing or excluding those microorganisms from wounds should prevent putrefaction — and therefore prevent the lethal infections that followed surgery.

The Carbolic Acid Experiments

Lister was aware that carbolic acid (phenol) was being used to treat sewage in Carlisle, England, with the result that fields treated with carbolic-treated sewage showed no signs of parasitic contamination. He reasoned it might work as an antiseptic for wounds.[5]

In August 1865, Lister treated an eleven-year-old boy — James Greenlees — who had been admitted to Glasgow Royal Infirmary with a compound fracture of the leg. In compound fractures, bone protrudes through the skin, and infection was nearly universal. Lister cleaned the wound with carbolic acid and dressed it with lint soaked in carbolic. The wound healed without infection. The boy kept his leg.[6]

Lister published his initial results in The Lancet in 1867 in a series of papers entitled "On the Antiseptic Principle in the Practice of Surgery." He reported eleven cases of compound fracture treated antiseptically, with just one death — and that from causes unrelated to infection.[7]

The Antiseptic Method

Lister's technique evolved over time. The core principle was to prevent microorganisms from entering wounds, and to kill those already present:

ElementLister's Approach
Wound cleaningCarbolic acid solution applied to wound and surrounding tissue
DressingsLint soaked in carbolic acid; later a multilayer "antiseptic dressing"
InstrumentsCarbolic acid used to clean instruments before and during surgery
Surgeon's handsWashed in carbolic acid solution before operating
Carbolic spray (later abandoned)Spraying the operating field with carbolic acid aerosol during surgery
SuturesDeveloped carbolised catgut sutures that could be left inside wounds to dissolve

Not all elements of Lister's method proved equally important. The carbolic spray, in particular, was eventually abandoned as unnecessarily cumbersome and potentially harmful. Later surgeons shifted from Lister's antiseptic approach (killing germs in the wound) to an aseptic approach (preventing germs from entering the operating field at all) — but the underlying principle remained Lister's.[8]

Reception and Adoption

Lister's methods met initial resistance, particularly in Britain where some senior surgeons remained sceptical of germ theory. Adoption was faster in Germany and the United States, where Listerian techniques were embraced enthusiastically. By the 1880s, Lister's principles had spread throughout the surgical world. Post-operative infection rates and mortality fell dramatically wherever the methods were adopted.[9]

Lister eventually received extensive recognition: he was appointed Surgeon-Extraordinary to Queen Victoria, created a baronet in 1883, and elevated to the peerage as Baron Lister of Lyme Regis in 1897. He was the first medical man to sit in the House of Lords. He died in 1912, widely regarded as one of the greatest surgeons in history.[3]

Lister, Semmelweis, and the Nature of Scientific Progress

Lister's success where Semmelweis had failed is instructive. Both men demonstrated, through clinical data, that hygiene practices dramatically reduced surgical and obstetric mortality. But Lister had something Semmelweis lacked: a theoretical explanation. Pasteur's germ theory gave Lister's evidence a framework. Physicians could understand why the method worked, not just that it worked. This made acceptance far more tractable.[10]

Lister was also a more effective communicator than Semmelweis — systematic in his publications, careful in his experimental method, and measured in responding to criticism. The contrast between the two men's fates illustrates that the social and rhetorical dimensions of science matter alongside the empirical ones.[10]
